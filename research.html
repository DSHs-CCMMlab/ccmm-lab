What We Do

Research

Computational Chemistry & Molecular Modelling Lab · Thapar University

We use the tools of quantum mechanics, statistical mechanics, and machine learning to understand how and why chemical reactions happen — from the electronic structure of a single metal complex to the dynamics of an entire enzyme active site.

Core Areas

Research Themes

Four interconnected pillars that define the scientific identity of the CCMM Lab.

01

Computational Chemistry & Molecular Modelling

At the heart of the lab lies the application of quantum chemical and classical simulation methods to model molecular systems at atomic resolution. Using Density Functional Theory (DFT), we probe the electronic structure, spin states, and reaction energetics of transition metal complexes — particularly high-valent metal–oxo species that are central to oxidation catalysis. Molecular Dynamics (MD) simulations extend these insights to the time domain, capturing conformational dynamics, solvation effects, and thermodynamic properties that govern real chemical behaviour. Together, these methods form a rigorous computational platform for understanding reactivity from first principles.

02

Theoretical Chemical Kinetics & Spectroscopy

A defining focus of the lab is understanding how quantum mechanical tunneling governs hydrogen atom transfer (HAT) in C–H bond cleavage reactions — a phenomenon that classical transition state theory cannot fully describe. We investigate tunneling probability, kinetic isotope effects (KIEs), and spin-crossover behaviour in iron(IV)–oxo and related systems to reveal the true quantum mechanical nature of these reactions. Complementing this, we study the effect of oriented external electric fields (OEEFs) as a non-chemical tool to tune reaction barriers and steer selectivity, a frontier area with deep implications for electrocatalysis and molecular engineering.

03

Environmental Chemistry & Radical Scavenging

The lab investigates the computational mechanisms by which organic compounds scavenge harmful free radicals — including hydroperoxyl (HOO·) and alkylperoxyl (CH₃OO·) radicals that play major roles in oxidative stress, atmospheric chemistry, and antioxidant activity. By mapping out hydrogen atom transfer, proton-coupled electron transfer, and radical addition pathways using DFT and kinetic modelling, we provide quantitative insight into how molecular structure dictates antioxidant potency. This work has direct relevance to the design of better antioxidant compounds and to understanding degradation processes in complex chemical environments.

04

Bioinspired Catalyst Design, Drug Design & Biomolecular Simulation

Drawing inspiration from natural metalloenzymes, we design and computationally evaluate synthetic catalysts that replicate — and potentially surpass — the efficiency and selectivity of biological systems for C–H functionalization. In parallel, the lab employs an integrated computational drug discovery pipeline: Molecular Docking screens large compound libraries against target proteins, Molecular Dynamics simulations validate binding stability under physiological conditions, Machine Learning models accelerate the prediction of bioactivity and ADMET properties, and pharmacophore filtering refines lead candidates. This end-to-end pipeline bridges quantum-level chemical insight with therapeutically relevant biological systems.

Active Work

Ongoing Projects

Current research efforts being actively pursued by the CCMM Lab team.

● Active

Metal–Oxo C–H Activation: DFT Mechanistic Studies

This project investigates the electronic structure and reactivity of high-valent metal–oxo intermediates in C–H bond activation reactions using Density Functional Theory. The work examines how the metal identity, oxidation state, spin state, and ligand environment collectively determine the barrier to hydrogen atom transfer and the degree of quantum tunneling contribution. Ongoing studies focus on Fe(IV)–oxo complexes with macrocyclic, porphyrinoid, and carboxylate-supported ligand frameworks, building a systematic understanding of how ligand architecture tunes reactivity and providing design principles for next-generation bioinspired oxidation catalysts.

● Active

Enzyme Catalysis: Integrated MD, Docking, ML & QM/MM Pipeline

This project employs a comprehensive multi-scale computational pipeline to study enzyme-catalyzed reactions and identify novel bioactive compounds. The workflow begins with large-scale virtual screening via Molecular Docking (AutoDock-GPU) to identify candidate molecules from chemical libraries. Promising hits are subjected to Molecular Dynamics simulations (AMBER24, GROMACS, NAMD) to assess binding stability, conformational flexibility, and free energetics under physiological conditions. Machine learning models trained on quantum chemical descriptors are used to filter and prioritize candidates based on predicted bioactivity and ADMET profiles. For the most promising systems, QM/MM calculations using ChemShell provide atomistic quantum mechanical detail on the catalytic mechanism within the full enzyme environment.
