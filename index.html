Dr. Debashish Mandal leads the Computational Chemistry & Molecular Modelling (CCMM) Lab at Thapar University. His research uncovers the quantum mechanical driving forces behind chemical reactivity — with a particular focus on tunneling-controlled C–H activation, the influence of oriented external electric fields on catalysis, and structure-based drug development. Using Density Functional Theory (DFT), he investigates the electronic structure and spin states of high-valent metal–oxo and transition metal complexes, revealing how subtle changes in ligand architecture dramatically alter oxidative reactivity. His group further integrates Machine Learning to accelerate property prediction across large chemical datasets, and employs Molecular Docking alongside QM/MM simulations to bridge quantum-level insights with biological targets — enabling the rational design of therapeutically relevant compounds. This research is supported by a DST INSPIRE Faculty Grant and has resulted in publications across top-tier journals including Dalton Transactions, Inorganic Chemistry, Biomacromolecules, and Journal of Organic Chemistry.

Research Focus

Core Areas

Three interconnected themes that define the lab's scientific identity.

01

Tunneling-Controlled C–H Activation

Investigating how quantum mechanical tunneling governs hydrogen atom transfer in C–H bond cleavage, particularly in Fe(IV)–oxo and related high-valent transition metal systems. Tunneling probability, spin-inversion, and kinetic isotope effects are central to this work.

02

Oriented External Electric Fields in Catalysis

Exploring how externally applied electric fields can be used as a "reagent" to tune reaction barriers, shift transition states, and steer chemical selectivity — a frontier area with implications for electrocatalysis and molecular machines.

03

Drug Design & Biomolecular Simulation

Using Molecular Docking, QM/MM, and MD simulations to investigate enzyme mechanisms and computationally design bioactive molecules, bridging quantum-level chemical insight with therapeutically relevant biological targets.
